HOW PLANTS RESPOND TO CHANGES IN WEATHER

The New York Times

THIS has been a fluky winter. In many ways it has seemed more like spring, because so many of the days have been mild. And where is that usual wintertime blizzard? Snow shovels and snow blowers have not had any workouts. The plants have reacted, too, if that is the word for it. Some have already bloomed. Many shrubs popped out with a few blooms last fall, particularly forsythias and lilacs. Some of the earliest bulbs have even pushed their leaves high above ground just as if it were spring. In some gardens the first of the witchhazels are in flower, and in others the snowdrops are out in full glory. Some gardeners have wondered if this sort of mild winter weather is what global warming is all about. Or is the Northeast caught in a peculiar pattern? Even the weather scientists are stumped for some sort of explanation. All of this speculation was quickly put to a halt, however, by the recent cold snap. Snap is just what it was. Temperatures dropped 30 to 40 degrees in a matter of a few hours. And without snow cover, nature's blanket, the plants were in a sort of shock, unprotected from these happenings.

The plants that endure the biggest challenge are the shrubs and trees, those elegant woody-stemmed monarchs that have their limbs and twigs exposed. These plants usually appear to tough it out through these kinds of challenges, because their buds are tightly wrapped. They are kept securely dormant through any vagaries the weather has to offer. As long as these large woody plants had a suitable supply of moisture, ample rainfall during the fall season, they should come through unscathed. One thing is certain, though, any flower buds that opened last fall have finished their display and will not have had time to re-form flowers for this spring. What buds have already opened to flower in fall cannot bloom again this spring. If some of the shrubs appear motley in flower as soon as genuine mild weather comes, this would explain why.

The plants that seem to be the hardest hit with this kind of cold snap are those that retain their leaves year-round, particularly the broadleaved evergreens. Rhododendrons, hollies, leucothoes, mountain laurels and pieris are among the popular landscape plants. All are subject to winter temperature stress and some more than others. Spring will tell. There is nothing gardeners can do now but keep their fingers crossed. Those who have rhododendrons on their property rarely need to consult a thermometer to know how cold it is. The rhododendrons have a weather gauge of their own. The colder it is outside, the tighter the leaves curl and hang down. There was a time when the temperature hovered near 10 degrees, and the rhododendron leaves, curled tightly inward, hung like long green pencils. When the temperatures began to ameliorate, toward 20 degrees, the leaves began to open a bit and look more like rhododendrons. But it was not until the thermometer was well above the freezing mark that the rhododendron leaves assumed their usual flat-out appearance and once again became the dramatic statement that these magnificent plants usually are. This phenomenon of nature is one of the best barometers of severe winter weather. And it is also one of the endowments of nature for a plant's winter survival. Few natural examples are as obvious as this one.
